Thermal Energy

Sample Text Here

Absolute Zero: The lowest possible temperature a substance can have (-273oC / 0K)

Gasses are comprised of many atoms (for example: a litre of air contains approximately 1025 atoms). As such, they follow rules of statistics very well and we can consider all the particles as following a distribution.

Atoms in a gas travel at a range of different speeds - this distribution is dependent on the temperature of the gas.

For example: a container of gas at room temperature contains many atoms - some of which are moving at 3ms-1, others moving at 400ms-1 but most of the atoms will have a speed somewhere inbetween these values (i.e. 150ms-1). These atoms that move very slowly have a temperature below the freezing point of the gas, however, the atoms at very high temperatures (i.e. 200oC) will balance out the overall temperature and speed of atoms.

Internal Energy: The sum of the randomly distributed kinetic and potential energies of all particles in a system.

Specific Heat Capacity

Specific Heat Capacity: The amount of energy needed to raise the temperature of 1kg of a substance by 1 Kelvin, without changing its state

Q = mcΔT

(Thermal Energy [J] = Mass [kg] x Specific Heat Capacity [Jkg-1K-1] x Change in Temperature [K])

A more useful expression of Specific Heat Capacity is:

Q = mc(Tf - Ti)

(Thermal Energy [J] = Mass [kg] x Specific Heat Capacity [Jkg-1K-1 or Jkg-1oC-1] x (Final Temperature [K or oC] - Initial Temperature [K or oC]))

As we are finding the change in temperature, it doesn't matter if we use Kelvin or degrees Celsius with this equation. As long as we are consistent and use Kelvin for both Tf and Ti, or degrees Celsius for Tf and Ti, the equation will work.

Specific Latent Heat

Specific Latent Heat: The amount of energy needed to change the state of 1kg of a substance without changing its temperature

Specific Latent Heat of fusion concerns the energy related to melting and freezing (solid-liquid)
Specific Latent Heat of vaporisation concerns the energy related to vaporisation and condensation (liquid-gas)
Solids can also transform into gasses (without first becoming a liquid), and vice versa through sublimation and deposition (solid-gas)

Gas Laws

Ideal gasses follow three gas laws by making use of some important assumptions. These laws were found empirically (meaning they were discovered from experiments in a lab)

Boyle's Law

Boyle's Law: For a fixed mass of gas, at constant temperature (T), pressure (p) is inversely proportional to volume (V)

  • Constant: N & T
  • p ∝ V-1

If the pressure and volume of a gas are inversely proportional, that means the product of pressure and volume is constant

  • pV = Constant

Charles's Law

Charles's Law: For a fixed mass of gas, at constant pressure (p), volume (V) is proportional to temperature (T)

  • Constant: N & p
  • V ∝ T

If the volume and temperature of a gas are proportional, that means the volume divided by the temperature is constant

  • V⁄T = Constant

Pressure Law

Pressure Law: For a fixed mass of gas, at constant volume (V), pressure (p) is proportional to temperature (T)

  • Constant: N & V
  • p ∝ T

If the pressure and temperature of a gas are proportional, that means the pressure divided by the temperature is constant

  • p⁄T = Constant

Ideal Gas Law

Ideal Gas: A theoretical gas composed of a large number of randomly moving particles that undergo perfectly elastic collisions. An ideal gas follows the gas laws perfectly

Ideal Gas Law: An equation that approximates the properties of an ideal gas. It is a comination of other empirical gas laws

PV = nRT

(Pressure [Pa] x Volume [m3] = No. of Moles [mol] x Molar Gas Constant [JK-1mol-1] x Temperature [K])

The Ideal Gas Law can also be written as:

PV = NkT

(Pressure [Pa] x Volume [m3] = No. of Molecules [] x Boltzmann Constant [JK-1] x Temperature [K])

We can treat any gas as an Ideal gas by following some assumptions

Ideal Gas Assumptions:

  • Molecules are small, hard spheres
  • Collisions are elastic & Newton's Laws of Motion apply
  • Energy is not lost in motion or collisions
  • Average distance between molecules is much larger than the size of molecues
  • Molecules move randomly with a distribution of speeds
  • Forces of attraction/repulsion don't exist between molecules
  • Long range forces don't exist between molecules and surroundings

Comparing two ideal gasses (1 & 2) with the same ...:

  • Number of molecules N, and Temperature T: P1V1 = P2V2
  • Number of molecules N, and Pressure p: V1⁄T1 = V2⁄T2
  • Number of molecules N, and Volume V: p1⁄T1 = p2⁄T2
  • Number of molecules N: p1V1⁄T1 = p2V2⁄T2

Definitions

Specific Heat Capacity: The amount of energy needed to raise the temperature of 1kg of a substance by 1 Kelvin, without changing its state

Specific Latent Heat: The amount of energy needed to change the state of 1kg of a substance without changing its temperature

Absolute Zero: The lowest possible temperature a substance can have (-273oC / 0K)

Ideal Gas: A Gas in which all collisions between atoms are perfectly elastic and there are no intermolecular attractive forces

Avogadro's Constant: The number of atoms in 12g of Carbon-12 (6.02×1023mol-1)

Molecular Mass: The sum of the masses of all the atoms that make up a molecule (also known as relative atomic mass)

Molar Mass: The mass of one mole of a substance (which is equal to its molecular mass when there is only one mole of the substance)

Mole: The amount of a substance containing NA particles

Boltzmann Constant: The gas constant of one molecule of gas (Molar Gas Constant divided by Avogadro's Constant)

Boyle's Law: For a fixed mass of gas, at constant temperature (T), pressure (p) is inversely proportional to volume (V)

Charles's Law: For a fixed mass of gas, at constant pressure (p), volume (V) is proportional to temperature (T)

Pressure Law: For a fixed mass of gas, at constant volume (V), pressure (p) is proportional to temperature (T)

Internal Energy: The sum of the randomly distributed kinetic and potential energies of all particles in a system

Root mean square (rms) speed: The average speed of particles in a gas
